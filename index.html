Projects

Byzantine Agreement

The Byzantine Agreement is a well-known problem in the field of distributed systems, which involves achieving consistent decision-making in the presence of a potentially untrustworthy element. The problem is named after the fictional historical event of the siege of Byzantium, in which three generals must decide whether to attack or retreat, but one of them may be a traitor attempting to sabotage the plan. It has been shown that classical methods cannot solve this problem, but quantum resources such as entangled many-qubit states can be used to design algorithms with high probability of success. In this project, we not only developed and analyzed one such protocol, but also conducted remote measurements on IBM's publicly available quantum computers. The result of our detailed analysis is published in this article [1].

Open Quantum Systems and Qubit Readout

An open quantum system is a quantum system that interacts with its environment, leading to decoherence in its state. The evolution of the state of such a system can be described using master equations, which are differential equations for the system's density matrix. During the readout of a qubit, the system is also an open system that is affected by its environment. Therefore, the measurement process itself must be described using master equations to accurately account for the system's interactions with its surroundings. One method for measuring a qubit is known as gate reflectometry, which involves using the system's effective capacitance and resistance upon a periodic drive to determine its state. This method has several benefits compared to conventional transport measurements. In this article [2] we examine a specific system and carry out gate reflectometry measurement in order to determine its properties.
